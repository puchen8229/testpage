Graph-Based Economic Models and Their Effectiveness in Interactive Learning Environments

 

Graph topology (ordered as roadmap)

Related economic concept(s)

Interactivity features and the economic issues they illuminate

Suggested controls, draggable elements, and check-answer features

1. Cross topology: one downward curve + one upward curve

Demand and supply equilibrium; non-price determinants; labour market equilibrium; factor-market basics

Shift either curve; compare old vs new equilibrium; optionally rotate slopes. Best for comparative statics, equilibrium adjustment, shortages/surpluses from shocks, and identifying whether a change is a movement along or a shift.

Controls: scenario dropdown, random scenario, reset, show/hide baseline equilibrium. Drag: demand and supply curves vertically or horizontally; optional slope handles. Check: which curve shifted, direction of shift, and direction of price/quantity change.

2. Cross topology with rotation around a fixed benchmark equilibrium

Elasticity; tax incidence; pass-through; responsiveness of quantity to shocks

Rotate demand or supply around the initial equilibrium so students isolate the effect of elasticity without moving the starting benchmark. Best for “who bears the burden?” and “which side is more elastic/inelastic?”

Controls: demand steepness slider, supply steepness slider, preset buttons for elastic/inelastic/perfectly inelastic cases. Drag: rotation handles on curves. Check: which side bears more of the burden; buyer share vs seller share; effect on quantity.

3. Cross topology with welfare shading and a wedge/parallel shift

Consumer surplus; producer surplus; economic surplus; taxes; subsidies; deadweight loss

Add a tax or subsidy wedge; shade CS, PS, government revenue/cost, and DWL before and after intervention. Best for linking policy to welfare decomposition.

Controls: intervention dropdown, amount slider, show/hide baseline areas, exact values on/off. Drag: tax wedge, shifted demand/supply curve. Check: signs and magnitudes of changes in CS, PS, government balance, total surplus, and DWL.

4. Cross topology with a horizontal policy line

Price ceiling; price floor; rent control; minimum wage; support price

Add a legal price line and allow realized quantity to differ from equilibrium. Best for binding vs non-binding controls, shortage/surplus logic, rationing, and welfare under controlled prices.

Controls: legal-price slider, preset cases, toggle binding/non-binding. Drag: horizontal price line; realized quantity marker. Check: whether the policy binds; shortage/surplus size; direction of surplus changes; correct realized quantity.

5. Cross topology with a vertical policy line

Quotas; quantity caps; fishing limits; emission caps; import quotas in partial equilibrium

Add a legal quantity line and allow realized price to move. Best for showing that quantity is fixed by policy while price adjusts, and for visualizing quota rents and DWL.

Controls: legal-quantity slider, quota-rent owner dropdown, show/hide feasible price band. Drag: vertical quota line; realized price line/point. Check: binding vs non-binding; quota rent size and recipient; changes in CS, PS, and DWL.

6. Cross topology with a world-price line and import/export gap

Small-country trade; tariff; import quota; export subsidy; domestic vs world price

Add a world price line, trade quantity, and policy wedges. Best for showing imports/exports, tariff revenue, quota rents, and the split between domestic and foreign market conditions.

Controls: world price slider, tariff/subsidy slider, quota toggle, import/export mode. Drag: world price line; quota line. Check: trade quantity, government revenue, domestic price after policy, and welfare changes.

7. Cross topology with private and social curves

Negative externalities; positive externalities; Pigouvian taxes/subsidies; socially efficient quantity

Add MPC vs MSC or MPB vs MSB and compare market equilibrium with social optimum. Best for overproduction/underproduction and corrective policy.

Controls: external cost/benefit slider, toggle private/social curves, policy slider for Pigouvian tax/subsidy. Drag: social-cost or social-benefit curve. Check: market vs efficient quantity; corrective tax/subsidy size; DWL removed or created.

8. Frontier topology: single bowed-out frontier

PPF; scarcity; efficiency; opportunity cost; economic growth; specialization

Move a point along, inside, or outside the frontier; shift the frontier outward or inward. Best for feasibility vs efficiency and changing opportunity cost.

Controls: technology/resource sliders, country/preset dropdown, trade line toggle. Drag: production point on the frontier; frontier shift handle. Check: feasible/unfeasible/efficient classification; opportunity-cost direction; gains from specialization.

9. Tangency topology: budget line + indifference map

Consumer choice; constrained optimization; normal/inferior goods; price and income changes

Pivot or shift the budget line and let students choose bundles. Best for understanding optimal choice as tangency and how prices/income affect demand.

Controls: income slider, price-of-X slider, price-of-Y slider, preference presets. Drag: budget line; chosen bundle. Check: whether the bundle is feasible; whether it is optimal; direction of change in each good after a price or income shock.

10. Tangency topology with old/new/compensated constraints

Income effect; substitution effect; Hicks/Slutsky decomposition; labour–leisure choice; intertemporal choice

Add old budget line, new budget line, and compensated line. Best for decomposing total change into substitution and income effects.

Controls: decomposition mode (Hicks/Slutsky), wage or interest-rate slider, endowment toggle. Drag: compensated budget line; chosen bundle. Check: sign of substitution effect, sign of income effect, and whether the good is normal/inferior.

11. Tangency topology: isoquant + isocost

Cost minimization; MRTS; input substitution; expansion path; long-run production choice

Shift or rotate isocost lines and change isoquants/output targets. Best for least-cost input mix and how factor-price changes alter input choice.

Controls: wage slider, rental-rate slider, output target slider, technology preset. Drag: isocost line; chosen input bundle. Check: least-cost tangency; whether labour/capital use rises or falls after factor-price changes.

12. Product-family topology: total, average, and marginal product curves

Diminishing marginal returns; stages of production; relationship among TP, AP, and MP

Move an input marker and dynamically trace TP, AP, and MP. Best for showing where MP peaks, where AP peaks, and the stages of production.

Controls: labour slider, technology shift button, fixed-factor size slider. Drag: input marker. Check: stage of production; where MP = AP; whether MP is rising or falling.

13. Cost-family topology: MC, AVC, ATC, AFC with a price line

Competitive firm in the short run; shutdown; break-even; profit/loss; firm supply

Add a price/MR line and highlight the profit-maximizing output. Best for linking cost curves to firm behaviours and supply decisions.

Controls: market price slider, fixed-cost slider, variable-cost/technology slider, show/hide profit rectangle. Drag: horizontal price line; output marker. Check: produce vs shut down; profit/loss sign; break-even vs shutdown point.

14. Market-power topology: demand, MR, MC, and ATC

Monopoly; monopoly vs competition; monopoly profit; regulation; monopoly DWL; simple price discrimination comparisons

Show MR below demand, let demand/MC move, and compare monopoly outcome with efficient or competitive outcome. Best for output restriction and welfare loss from market power.

Controls: market-structure toggle, regulation mode, demand elasticity slider, cost sliders. Drag: demand and MC curves; regulated price line. Check: monopoly quantity and price; profit; DWL; comparison with competitive benchmark.

15. Factor-market-power topology: labor supply to the firm, MFC, and MRP

Monopsony; labor-market power; minimum wage under monopsony

Add an upward-sloping labor supply to the firm and MFC above it; optionally add a minimum-wage line. Best for showing underemployment and the special minimum-wage case.

Controls: labour-supply steepness slider, MRP shift slider, minimum-wage toggle/slider. Drag: wage floor line; labor-supply handle. Check: wage and employment changes; whether minimum wage raises or lowers employment.

16. Strategy-space topology: reaction functions in quantity or price space

Cournot; Stackelberg; best responses; Nash equilibrium; strategic substitutes/complements

Plot firm 1 and firm 2 reaction curves and allow quantity choices to move. Best for strategic interaction and equilibrium as mutual best response.

Controls: demand intercept slider, marginal-cost sliders, leader/follower mode. Drag: output choices; reaction curves if parameterized. Check: Nash equilibrium point; leader vs follower outcome; profit comparison.

17. Box topology: Edgeworth box with contract curve

Exchange; gains from trade; Pareto efficiency; competitive equilibrium; welfare theorems

Move endowment and allocation points inside a box with two agents’ indifference curves. Best for mutual gains from trade and the idea of efficient allocations.

Controls: utility-parameter sliders, endowment presets, price-line toggle. Drag: endowment point; current allocation point. Check: Pareto efficient or not; whether both parties gain from trade; location relative to contract curve.

18. Aggregation topology: vertically summed benefit curves + MC

Public goods; efficient provision; free-rider problem

Add individual MB curves and stack them vertically into social MB. Best for why public goods are not analysed like private goods.

Controls: preference sliders for each person, number-of-agents presets, MC slider. Drag: quantity marker; individual MB curves. Check: efficient quantity; difference between private and social willingness to pay; free-rider gap.

19. Risk-choice topology: utility curve over wealth and gamble points

Risk aversion; certainty equivalent; risk premium; insurance choice; expected utility

Move payoff/probability points and show expected value vs expected utility. Best for why concavity of utility implies insurance demand.

Controls: probability sliders, wealth-outcome sliders, utility-curvature slider, insurance premium slider. Drag: lottery outcomes; certainty-equivalent point. Check: EV vs CE vs risk premium; whether insurance is worth buying.

20. Contract-space topology: indifference curves, isoprofit lines, and constraints

Adverse selection; signalling; screening; principal-agent; incentive compatibility

Plot contracts in outcome space and show participation and incentive-compatibility constraints. Best for upper-level information economics.

Controls: type-probability slider, outside-option slider, effort-cost slider, technology/monitoring slider. Drag: contract point; separating/pooling menu. Check: which constraints bind; pooling vs separating; incentive-insurance trade-off.

21. Cross topology: AD downward, SRAS upward, LRAS vertical

Static AD–AS; inflationary and recessionary gaps; demand shocks vs supply shocks; fiscal and monetary stabilization

Shift AD, SRAS, or LRAS; compare old vs new equilibrium; optionally show the output gap and unemployment implication. Best for distinguishing demand-side vs supply-side shocks and short run vs long run.

Controls: shock dropdown, AD/SRAS/LRAS sliders, expected inflation toggle, baseline on/off. Drag: AD, SRAS, LRAS. Check: which curve shifted, direction of real GDP, price level, unemployment, and whether the shock is temporary or structural.

22. Moving-cross topology: dynamic AD–AS path over time

Dynamic AD–AS; trend growth; inflation dynamics; balanced growth vs overheating vs disinflation

Animate AD, SRAS, and LRAS shifting over time; let students see how inflation depends on whether AD grows faster or slower than productive capacity. Best for teaching growth with inflation control rather than one-off shocks.

Controls: population growth, productivity growth, money/demand growth, inflation target, play/pause, speed. Drag: growth rates of AD/SRAS/LRAS or a target path. Check: whether inflation rises, falls, or stays stable; whether the economy returns to potential; which curve must shift faster for price stability.

23. 45-degree topology: expenditure line vs 45-degree line

Keynesian cross; multiplier; autonomous spending; inventory adjustment; leakages

Shift the aggregate expenditure line and change its slope; reveal the multiplier and the gap between planned and actual expenditure. Best for short-run demand determination and why spending shocks move output.

Controls: MPC, autonomous C/I/G/NX, taxes, imports, transfer payments. Drag: expenditure intercept and slope. Check: equilibrium income, multiplier size, direction of inventory change, and whether the shock is autonomous or induced.

24. Cross topology in (Y, i): IS downward, LM upward

IS–LM; fiscal policy, monetary policy, crowding out, closed-economy demand management

Shift IS and LM; show the new equilibrium interest rate and output; optionally derive AD from repeated policy moves. Best for policy mix and interest-rate/output trade-offs.

Controls: G, T, M, P, money-demand sensitivity, investment sensitivity. Drag: IS and LM. Check: direction of Y and i, extent of crowding out, and whether policy is expansionary or contractionary.

25. Linked-panel / modern policy topology: IS–MP–PC or IS–PC–MR

3-equation New Keynesian model; inflation targeting; output gap; policy rule; modern central-bank analysis

Link an IS block, a Phillips-curve block, and a monetary-policy-rule block; let one shock propagate through all three. Best for modern monetary policy logic and why the central bank reacts to inflation and output gaps rather than “money” directly.

Controls: inflation target, neutral real rate, Taylor-rule coefficients, shock persistence, credibility/expectations toggle. Drag: policy-rule line or current output-gap point. Check: path of inflation, output, and policy rate; whether the rule stabilizes or destabilizes the economy.

26. Downward-curve topology: Phillips curve / wage-price spiral

Phillips curve; expected inflation; natural rate / NAIRU; disinflation; short-run trade-off vs long-run vertical relation

Move along the curve, then shift the curve with expected inflation; show short-run vs long-run versions. Best for trade-off intuition, expectations, and sacrifice-ratio style thinking.

Controls: expected inflation, NAIRU, supply shock, credibility parameter. Drag: current macro point and expected-inflation anchor. Check: movement vs shift, short-run vs long-run interpretation, and whether inflation persistence increases or decreases.

27. Cross topology: money market or loanable-funds market

Interest-rate determination via money/loanable-funds diagrams; transmission of monetary policy to borrowing costs and investment

Shift money supply/demand or loanable-funds supply/demand; compare interest-rate outcomes. Best for basic transmission channels and for contrasting frameworks used in intro courses.

Controls: nominal money supply, liquidity preference, income, inflation expectations, savings/investment shifters. Drag: supply and demand curves. Check: direction of interest-rate change, change in lending/borrowing, and which side moved.

28. Labor-market topology in (real wage, employment): WS–PS or macro labour market

Wage setting / price setting, natural unemployment, markup effects, bargaining power, and the supply-side foundation of inflation dynamics

Shift WS or PS and map the result into unemployment and real wages; optionally link the result to SRAS or the Phillips curve. Best for connecting labour institutions to medium-run macro outcomes.

Controls: markup, unemployment benefits, bargaining power, productivity, labour-force size. Drag: WS and PS. Check: natural unemployment, real wage, employment, and whether the shock is institution- or productivity-driven.

29. Vacancy–unemployment topology: Beveridge curve

Matching frictions, cyclical vs structural unemployment, labour-market efficiency

Move a point along the curve in a cycle; shift the curve when matching efficiency changes. Best for distinguishing recessionary unemployment from mismatch / structural unemployment.

Controls: matching efficiency, separation rate, recruiting intensity, vacancy posting cost. Drag: current point and Beveridge curve position. Check: movement vs shift, cyclical vs structural interpretation, and whether policy should target demand or matching efficiency.

30. Concave-growth topology: Solow diagram

Solow model; capital accumulation; steady state; Golden Rule; effects of saving, depreciation, population growth, and technology

Shift the investment curve or break-even line; animate convergence to a new steady state. Best for seeing how growth level effects differ from growth-rate effects.

Controls: saving rate, depreciation, population growth, technology growth, productivity level. Drag: steady-state marker or investment schedule. Check: new steady state, transition direction, and whether consumption rises or falls in the long run.

31. Cross topology in FX space: currency demand and supply

Exchange-rate determination; appreciation/depreciation; trade and capital-flow shocks

Shift demand or supply of the domestic currency; reveal the implied exchange-rate move and net-export effect. Best for building intuition before Mundell–Fleming.

Controls: foreign interest rate, capital inflow, export demand, import demand, expectations. Drag: FX demand and supply curves. Check: appreciation vs depreciation, impact on NX, and whether the driver is trade or finance.

32. Linked open-economy policy topology: Mundell–Fleming / IS–LM–BP / IS–LM**

Open-economy stabilization; fixed vs floating exchange rates; capital mobility; fiscal vs monetary effectiveness

Toggle the exchange-rate regime, then apply fiscal or monetary shocks and watch output and the exchange rate respond. Best for the classic result that policy effectiveness depends on the exchange-rate regime.

Controls: exchange-rate regime, capital mobility, world interest rate, fiscal shock, monetary shock. Drag: IS*/LM* and the regime constraint. Check: which policy is powerful under fixed vs floating rates, and direction of Y and e.

33. Time-path / impulse-response topology

RBC, DSGE, and New Keynesian dynamics; shock persistence; overshooting; transition paths

Show impulse responses of output, inflation, employment, and interest rates after a shock; let students change persistence or rigidity. Best for moving from static diagrams to modern dynamic macro intuition.

Controls: shock size, persistence, price stickiness, Taylor-rule aggressiveness, technology vs demand shock type. Drag: shock date/size or persistence. Check: sign, peak timing, persistence, and half-life of each response.

34. Debt-dynamics topology: debt ratio path / stabilizing-balance locus

Government debt dynamics, debt sustainability, r–g arithmetic, primary balances, automatic stabilizers

Move the economy through debt-ratio space or simulate a time path under different fiscal rules. Best for distinguishing temporary deficits from unstable debt arithmetic.

Controls: initial debt/GDP, interest rate, growth rate, primary balance, cyclical sensitivity. Drag: primary-balance path or debt starting point. Check: stable vs unstable debt path, debt-stabilizing primary balance, and effect of a growth slowdown.

35. Accounting-quadrant topology: MMT sectoral balances

MMT / stock-flow-consistent style accounting; government balance, private domestic balance, external balance; functional-finance intuition

Place a point for any two balances and automatically infer the third using the accounting identity. Best for showing that all sectors cannot run surpluses at once and that one sector’s deficit is another’s surplus.

Controls: G, T, private saving desire, investment, trade balance, % of GDP display. Drag: government-balance point and external-balance point. Check: whether balances sum to zero, implied private balance, and whether a chosen combination is impossible.

36. Flow-map / balance-sheet topology: MMT-style monetary operations and endogenous money

Treasury–central bank–bank–private sector operations; endogenous money; reserve creation; bond issuance; QE; lender of last resort

Click through transactions and watch balance sheets update in sequence. Best for clarifying who creates deposits, what deficits add to private net financial assets, and how asset swaps differ from deficit spending.

Controls: deficit spending, tax payment, bond sale, QE purchase, bank loan, interest on reserves. Drag: transaction arrows or account balances. Check: accounting consistency, change in reserves vs deposits vs bonds, and whether net financial assets rose or merely changed form.

37. Buffer-stock / anchor topology: MMT Job Guarantee

Job Guarantee / employer of last resort as a nominal anchor, buffer stock of employed labour, stabilization of employment and prices

Let private employment and the Job Guarantee pool expand and contract with demand shocks; show the anchor wage and inflation pressure. Best for teaching the MMT claim that a Job Guarantee can stabilize both employment and the price anchor.

Controls: JG wage, private-demand shock, hiring responsiveness, tax rate, productivity. Drag: JG wage line or private-demand schedule. Check: private vs JG employment, inflation pressure, and whether the anchor is stabilizing or destabilizing.
